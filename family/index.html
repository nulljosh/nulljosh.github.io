Limitation analysis
This case involves multiple incidents across 20+ years. Limitation is the primary defense defendants will use.
Childhood acts (age 10, 15, infancy):
BC Limitation Act s.16 suspends limitation during minority. Clock started at 19 (~2019). Basic 2-year expired ~2021. These are dead on basic limitation. Viable only via: (1) s.8(1)(d) discoverability — first knew of claim May 2026; (2) s.21 ultimate limit — 15 years from act — most already past 15 years.
Likeness/appropriation:
Ongoing tort if still in commercial use. 2-year clock from last use date or from discovery. Strongest head on limitation.
Eviction + parking lot:
2-year from incident. If within 2024–2026, basic limitation still open. If earlier, discoverability argument applies.
Strategy: lead with likeness (cleanest limitation) + IIMS from recent acts. Use childhood incidents as context for damages and causation — not as separate causes of action unless discoverability argument holds.

Per-head damages stack
Asset enforcement
Unlike CASE-0001 (government defendant — deep pockets by definition), this case is against private individuals. Enforcing a judgment matters.

Known assets: Primary residence ($1M+ Langley home, owned per recordings). Family business (trucks, equipment, revenue). Retirement savings (defendants are ~60, retired).

Key question for counsel: Is the home in both parents' names? Is the business incorporated? Is equity accessible for judgment enforcement in BC? A judgment against parents can be registered against the home title (BC Law and Equity Act).

Family home exemption: BC does not have a homestead exemption for judgment debtors in the same way some US states do. A registered judgment can force a sale if they do not pay.

Strategy
Lead with the likeness claim. It is the cleanest, has the strongest limitation argument (ongoing tort), and is provable with photographs. Build from there.
Do not file anything until you have counsel. Admissions in pre-retainer communications (voicemails, texts, social media) can be used against you. Clean up your digital footprint before filing.
Priority actions:
1. Photograph every vehicle, truck, or material with your face on it — with timestamps
2. Screenshot any online presence (website, social, Google My Business) with your image
3. Document the homelessness period — dates, locations, anyone who witnessed it
4. Record any ongoing contact with parents (keep it minimal and factual)
5. Do NOT confront parents about the lawsuit before filing

Risks — what defendants will attack
Limitation (kill shot). Defendants will file to strike on limitation. Every childhood claim is vulnerable. The discoverability argument must be documented, specific, and contemporaneous. A court will ask: when exactly did you first understand you had a legal remedy? May 2026 must be supported by evidence.
IIMS evidentiary bar. Intentional infliction of mental suffering requires proving the conduct was calculated to harm AND caused a recognized psychiatric illness. Defendants will argue their acts were reasonable parenting. Need expert psychiatric evidence connecting the harm to specific acts.
Likeness consent. Defendants may argue implied consent — Joshua knew about the business and didn't object at the time. Counter: any consent given as a minor is invalid. Document when the use began relative to age.
Causation splitting. PTSD has two potential causes: RCMP incident (CASE-0001) and family environment. Defendants will argue all PTSD is from RCMP. Need psychiatric expert who can attribute portion of harm to each cause independently.
Battery (circumcision). Novel argument in Canada. No appellate authority directly on point. Defendants will argue parental consent is valid. High-risk, high-reward. Include as supplementary, not lead claim.
