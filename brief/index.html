Charter Rights Brief
▼

1. Statement of Facts

Police attended on a wellness call. Officers entered without a warrant and without consent. No probable cause for a criminal investigation existed.

Walking away from police during a non-criminal wellness check does not constitute flight and does not authorize the use of force.

Officers physically restrained the subject by kneeling on his back. He was transported, forcibly medicated without consent, and held in solitary confinement overnight. Documented PTSD resulted.

2. Grounds of Action

Ground 1 — Unlawful Entry (s.8)

R. v. Feeney [1997] 2 SCR 13: warrantless entry into a dwelling violates s.8. A Feeney warrant is required. A wellness call provides no basis. No exigent circumstances existed.

Ground 2 — Arbitrary Detention (s.9)

No lawful grounds to detain. Walking away is not flight. Detention arbitrary from inception. R. v. Grant [2009] 2 SCR 353.

Ground 3 — Excessive Force (s.7)

Kneeling on subject's back causing inability to breathe. Unjustifiable under any scenario. Elmardy v. TPSB, 2019 ONSC 2931 — significant Charter damages for excessive force.

Ground 4 — Forced Medication (s.7)

Medicated without consent. Absolute right to refuse treatment under s.7. Charter breach and tort of battery. Fleming v. Ontario [2019] SCC.

Ground 5 — Solitary Confinement (ss.7, 12)

Overnight solitary following unlawful arrest, without charge and without counsel. Violates liberty (s.7) and prohibits cruel and unusual treatment (s.12).

Ground 6 — Punitive / Egregious Conduct

Not a good-faith mistake. Escalation from wellness call to forcible entry, restraint, forced medication, and solitary supports punitive damages.
