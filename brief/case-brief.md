# Case Brief — Charter Violations re: Warrantless Wellness Call Entry

**Jurisdiction:** British Columbia, Canada  
**Date of Incident:** TBD  
**Prepared by:** Joshua Trommel  
**Status:** Pre-litigation — lawyer consultation pending

---

## 1. Statement of Facts

Police attended the residence on a wellness call — a request to check on a person's wellbeing, not to investigate a crime. Officers entered the dwelling without a warrant and without consent. No probable cause for a criminal investigation existed at the time of entry.

Upon encountering the subject, officers pursued him after he walked away. Walking away from police during a non-criminal wellness check does not constitute flight, does not create reasonable grounds for arrest, and does not authorize the use of force.

Officers physically restrained the subject by kneeling on his back, causing inability to breathe. He was then transported and forcibly medicated without consent. He was held in solitary confinement overnight.

The subject has documented PTSD as a direct result of this incident, which he has identified as the most traumatic event of his life.

---

## 2. Grounds of Action

### Ground 1 — Unlawful Entry (s.8 Charter)

Police entered a private dwelling without a warrant and without probable cause.

**Authority: R. v. Feeney [1997] 2 SCR 13**

The Supreme Court of Canada held that the common law rule permitting warrantless entry into a dwelling to effect an arrest is inconsistent with s.8 of the Charter. A Feeney warrant is required before police may enter a private home to make an arrest. A wellness call does not constitute a criminal investigation and provides no basis for warrantless entry. The entry was unlawful from the moment officers crossed the threshold.

The subject did not consent to entry. No exigent circumstances existed (no fire, no active crime in progress, no hot pursuit from a criminal event).

### Ground 2 — Arbitrary Detention (s.9 Charter)

The subject was detained without lawful grounds. The wellness call provided no authority to detain, arrest, or restrain. Walking away from officers on a wellness call is not grounds for arrest. The detention was arbitrary — it lacked a legal basis from inception.

### Ground 3 — Excessive Force / Security of the Person (s.7 Charter)

Officers knelt on the subject's back, causing him to be unable to breathe. This goes beyond any force that could be justified even in a lawful arrest scenario, and is categorically unjustifiable given the unlawful nature of the detention.

**Supporting authority: Elmardy v. Toronto Police Services Board, 2019 ONSC 2931** — excessive force during arrest resulted in significant Charter damages award.

### Ground 4 — Forced Medication / Bodily Autonomy (s.7 Charter)

The subject was medicated without consent. Competent adults have an absolute right to refuse medical treatment under s.7. Forcible medication without consent and without a lawful court order constitutes both a Charter breach and the tort of battery.

### Ground 5 — Solitary Confinement (s.7 and s.12 Charter)

Overnight solitary confinement following an unlawful arrest, without charge and without access to counsel, violates both the right to liberty (s.7) and the right not to be subjected to cruel and unusual treatment (s.12).

### Ground 6 — Punitive / Egregious Conduct

The conduct was not a good-faith mistake. Officers attended on a wellness call — explicitly a helping role — and escalated to forcible entry, physical restraint, forced medication, and solitary confinement. The pattern of conduct is egregious and supports an award of punitive damages.

## 3. Applicable Law

| Case | Principle |
|------|-----------|
| R. v. Feeney [1997] 2 SCR 13 | Police require a Feeney warrant to enter a dwelling to make an arrest; warrantless entry violates s.8 |
| Ward v. Vancouver (City) [2010] 2 SCR 28 | Framework for Charter damages under s.24(1); damages available for police misconduct |
| Elmardy v. Toronto Police Services Board, 2019 ONSC | Excessive force + Charter violations = significant damages award |
| Fleming v. Ontario [2019] SCC | s.7 protects against state actions that restrict bodily autonomy |
| R. v. Grant [2009] 2 SCR 353 | Test for s.9 arbitrary detention |
